Recovering mistaken payments and dealing with the risk of dissipation

21 March 2011 | in Dispute Resolution, Featured Articles

By Ahsan Khan, Solicitor

Mistaken payments equate to millions of pounds a year in the UK and can create a substantial loss and headache for lenders.

In November 2009, The Payment Services Regulations 2009 (“Regulations”), came into force and applied the Payment Services Directive in the UK.

Under the Regulations, lenders must immediately refund a customer for any unauthorised or incorrectly executed transaction which, if not handled correctly, can result in a substantial loss to the bank or building society.  

Often, it is the lender that makes the mistake, for example, a payment may be sent to a recipient twice or even to the wrong recipient.  In this instance, the lender can recover the mistaken payment directly from the recipient. 

In cases where the recipient has dissipated the money, or there is a real risk this could happen, the lender has the following options:

Freezing order

A freezing order prevents a party from moving or dealing with certain assets.  Once a freezing order is obtained, the lender can commence court proceedings to obtain judgment against the unintended recipient and enforce it. 

The court may grant a freezing order if the bank or building society can prove there is a good and arguable case against the unintended recipient and that there is a ‘real risk’ of the unintended recipient dissipating the money.

It is important to note that a freezing order does not give a right over the asset, but merely prevents the asset from being dissipated.  In addition it is a time consuming and expensive process and the applicant is required to guarantee various undertakings to the court, including that it will pay costs to the respondent or a third party if the freezing order should not have been granted. 

Tracing claim

If it is difficult to show a risk of dissipation, the lender could trace the mistaken payment through bank accounts, particularly where the mistaken payment is mixed up with other money.  The lender must prove that it has a good arguable case to show it owns the assets in question, or that the assets can be traced back to the lender.

A tracing claim is essentially a process to obtain an equitable charge or lien over the payment to allow the lender to recover it.   The lender is unable to do a tracing claim if the mistaken payment is used to discharge a debt, for example where the mistaken payment is used to pay an overdraft.

Search order

This court order enables a party to enter the other party’s premises to preserve evidence that may be concealed or destroyed.  To obtain a search order, the lender must prove:

  • it has a strong prima facie case;
  • that the potential or actual damage to the bank or building society is very serious;
  • clear evidence that the recipient has assets or documents in their possession that would give rise to liability and there is a real risk that it would be destroyed.

As with freezing orders, the applicant is required to guarantee various undertakings to the court including that it will pay costs to the respondent for any loss caused.  The search order must be conducted under the supervision of an independent and experienced solicitor.

These options are often used simultaneously before the commencement of legal proceedings and can prove useful in identifying and preserving assets.

Lenders should implement processes that prevent mistaken payments or have audit procedures to identify mistaken payments quickly.  On identifying mistaken payments, lenders must act expeditiously to prevent the risk of dissipation and increase the chance of getting the money back.
