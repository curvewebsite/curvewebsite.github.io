Banks lose battle against the FSA over PPI rules in the High Court

21 April 2011 | in Featured Articles, Litigation
by Nicola Hoskins, Head of Learning and Development

Mr Justice Ouseley has dismissed the judicial review brought on behalf of the British Bankers Association (“BBA”) and its members against the FSA’s complaints handling guidance around the mis-selling of payment protection insurance.    

PPI has historically been sold alongside associated credit products, and it is designed to pay the instalments of the loan or other commitment where the policyholder’s income reduces on account of unemployment or illness. However, allegations of mis-selling have arisen on an enormous scale with claimants stating that they were unaware that they had been sold the cover, or that the cover proved to be inappropriate for their situation when they tried to claim.

The FSA stepped into the PPI arena after it noted that at least 80% of related complaints which were referred to the Financial Ombudsman Service (“FOS”) were upheld. It concluded that banks’ internal processes dealing with PPI were not adequate, and sought to bring the complaints procedures of financial institutions more in line with the approach of the FOS.       

The FSA guidance came into force in December 2010. The BBA sought to challenge the rules on the basis that the new principles and guidance should not apply retrospectively. This, the BBA argued, would see the application of new standards to cases which pre-dated the rules and would force them to review past cases even where customers had not complained. It was argued that effectively, the imposition of the new rules was a “back door review”, and there were fears that such an approach may open the floodgates to other retrospective reviews of other financial products. However, the court appears to have accepted the FSA’s argument that the spirit of the new principles and guidance did not fundamentally alter the previous position as banks and other sellers of PPI were under a duty to comply with the wider ICOB rules in the sale of insurance products, as well as to treat customers fairly in any event.   

In terms of numbers, the FOS has dealt with more than 200,000 PPI cases, and this continues to rise: since the judicial review proceedings were issued in October 2010, it has been receiving up to 5,000 referrals a week. At least part of the reason for this surge was the BBA’s advice to its members that complaints which related to matters raised in the judicial review could be put on hold until the outcome – the FSA, in an open letter, criticised this approach and made it clear that consumers could refer matters to the FOS if no final response had been received within 8 weeks.     

The potential cost to the industry has been estimated in the proceedings at £4.5 billion, and will inevitably mean the folding of some financial services firms. This in turn would have an impact on the Financial Services Compensation Scheme, who will ultimately have to deal with any resulting refunds. 

Natalie Ceeney, chief ombudsman, added: “This judgment is very clear-cut and confirms that the ombudsman’s approach to PPI complaints is right. People have been waiting a long time while the banks’ legal action has been ongoing. I would now like to see financial businesses showing real commitment to sorting out their customers’ complaints efficiently and promptly.”

A spokesman for the BBA said: “We are disappointed with today’s judgment and now need to consider the details of it very carefully, as well as next steps including whether it would be appropriate to apply for permission to appeal.”

He went on: “any complaints that are directly affected by the judicial review, and therefore cannot be decided, will continue to be placed on hold until the next steps have been decided. We will continue to work closely with the FSA to ensure that all complaints are appropriately handled and customers are not disadvantaged. Customers who are considering making a complaint or who have a specific query about an existing complaint should contact their bank in the first instance.”

The judgment does not give the BBA permission to appeal – it has until 10 May 2011 to apply to the Court of Appeal if it decides to take this option. It will also have to consider how it advises its members to proceed in the interim, given the comments made in the judgment today. When added to the upcoming ban on the sale of PPI products at the point of sale, the whole landscape appears to have changed. But is PPI finished? Watch this space.
