Rehabilitation – A modern approach

18 April 2011 | in Featured Articles, Repossession
by Richard Taylor, Associate and Head of Property Litigation

There can be many reasons why a mortgage falls into arrears.  After all, very few people take on the responsibility of a mortgage with the intention of defaulting on payments, which could ultimately result in the loss of their home.  Understanding these reasons and working with the defaulting borrower undoubtedly presents the best opportunity a lender has to rehabilitate defaulted accounts and turn things around for the benefit of all parties concerned.

Historically rehabilitation was not a priority for lenders.  The focus was on the asset, rather than the borrower.  House prices were rising and negative equity was the exception rather than the norm. Once a mortgage was in arrears sufficient to justify the commencement of proceedings, the instruction to the solicitor was to seek repossession of the property as quickly as possible on the basis that the loss could be recouped through the property sale.

For several reasons however, this approach has now been abandoned in favour of a more customer focused approach to the issue of mortgage arrears.

The introduction of the pre-action protocol for mortgage arrears has ensured that lenders must explore all opportunities to agree a repayment schedule with the borrower before commencing proceedings.   Lenders must be able to evidence their compliance with the protocol before the court if they seek an order for possession. If they cannot, the order will not be granted.

Further, the introduction of the Treating Customers Fairly (“TCF”) principles has also been a major factor in prompting a review of how defaulting borrowers are now managed.

All of this change has also coincided with a recession where the majority of properties taken into possession are now in negative equity, with the result that lenders would suffer losses should properties be repossessed and sold.

So, how have things changed?

Attempts at rehabilitation cannot stop once solicitors are instructed. An increasing number of lenders now require their solicitors to actively engage with the borrowers to seek a workable solution to the mortgage arrears, rather than simply obtain possession of the borrower’s property.

The reason for this is simple. The key to any successful rehabilitation strategy is to maintain dialogue with the borrower. Some borrowers, who have been unwilling to engage with the lender will respond to a solicitor’s letter or phone call, perhaps when they realise the seriousness of their situation. The solicitor therefore has perhaps a better opportunity to attempt rehabilitation with the borrower than the lender had previously.

The emphasis on solicitors has therefore changed in line with the lender’s requirements from taking possession proceedings to treating possession as an absolute last resort. This has required solicitors to actively embrace the principles of rehabilitation and set up effective teams and processes to try and engage with borrowers.  The challenge for solicitors is how to achieve contact and seek an appropriate repayment plan with a defaulting borrower who has already been through a collection strategy with the lender prior to their instruction.

As the solicitor is effectively an extension of the lender it is also important that an effective rehabilitation strategy is developed that takes account of the various regulations and codes as prescribed by the FSA, the OFT and MCOB.

The skill set required of a person who is involved with the rehabilitation of a borrower in arrears is different to that of a litigator.  At Optima Legal therefore, we have established a specialist team who focus solely on the rehabilitation of our client’s borrowers. Borrowers are more likely to prioritise a mortgage debt, if given the right support and guidance, as well as ease of access to trained and experienced negotiators at an early stage. Our rehabilitation team are governed by our own code of practice, which provides guidance and information on how to deal with customers who are experiencing mortgage payment difficulty. In addition, we also offer extended opening hours and a SMS text messaging service to maximise contact opportunities with borrowers.

We have also worked closely with the national debt advice charity, Money Advice Trust (“MAT”), to develop our approach to customer rehabilitation and to ensure that we remain at the forefront of any developments or government initiatives that will help borrowers in financial difficulty.

Joanna Elson OBE, Chief Executive of MAT commented “Optima Legal has taken a positive step in the right direction, working towards best practice, and that is something we applaud. It is important for any organisation working with borrowers struggling with their finances to carefully consider rehabilitation practices in order to ensure their clients receive the best possible support and protection. We are pleased Optima Legal commits to this challenge.”

Richard Taylor, Product Head – Property Litigation, Optima Legal commented “We are aware of the financial difficulties many people find themselves in, often through no fault of their own. At Optima Legal we are committed to embracing the principles of rehabilitation and ensuring that repossession is treated as an absolute last resort.  In order to achieve this, it is important to have in place a dedicated rehabilitation team who are empathetic to the borrower’s circumstances. We believe that our approach to rehabilitation provides the best opportunity at avoiding repossession and maintaining the customer/lender relationship, thus providing significant benefits to both our lender clients and their borrowers.”
