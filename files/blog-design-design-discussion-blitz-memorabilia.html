Design Discussion - Blitz Memorabilia

Matt Saunders
Wednesday, 21st November, 2012

This weekend I had the opportunity to visit the Discovery Museum in the wonderful city of Newcastle. The museum features a range of exhibitions from engineering and history, through to science and art. Some of the pieces are interactive and purpose-built, others prohibited from touch due to their historic value. The aim of every exhibition however, is the same; to inspire, discover and teach.

The idea for this article was actually spawned from the most unlikely of places - the museum gift shop. When thrifting through the obligatory postcards, t-shirts and debating whether to buy a souvenir mug or not, I happened across a Blitz Replica Pack which contained a collection of photocopied memorabilia from World War Two, specifically the years 1940-1941. It immediately struck me that, despite this being a terrible time in Human history, some care and attention had gone into the design of this material. Despite the fact that the purpose of these cards and booklets was to provide guidance in a life or death situation, some thought had been given to typography, legibility and use of colour. Also, despite the fact that there was probably not a lot of money to be made from the commissioning of this material, it still remained fit for purpose and could be replicated on a large scale. Giving additional kudos to the various groups of people behind this, they probably had little time to organise, create and publish this material.

The materials...

After the Raid leaflet

The inner pages of this booklet remain quite uninspired, but I do feel captivated by the cover. The bold typeface sandwiched between the stretched diamond lines give it something of a prestigious feel; that is before you consider the chilling subject matter at hand.

Identity card

Much like the After the Raid leaflet, the identity card is somewhat chaotic once you open it up, but the cover provides very concise information communicated using a clear typeface. The variation of size and style create a hierarchy. Personally, I find my eyes drawn to the title and then moving down to the italic, serif typeface at the bottom.

Crush injury tag

Probably the most morbid in the collection, the injury tag had been created for the purpose of attaching to unconscious casualties and dead bodies. Imagine being asked to design something like this - where would you start? This tag is effectively a form on which the doctor can provide details of the patient - identifying their medication dosage, address and apparent cause of death/injury.

ARP booklet (seventh edition)

This Air Raid Precautions booklet includes a colour cover and is designed to offer a practical guide to surviving air raids. Alongside the advice for building your own bomb shelter, the booklet also appears to include advertisements for companies which sell gas masks and firefighting equipment - proving that there is always the opportunity to capitalise in even the most dire of situations!

What can we take from all of this?

I think the loudest message to be heard from examining this material is that it reaffirms the idea that design is a very powerful tool, and it’s subtleties can be used to dramatically change how a message is communicated. In the case of much of this material, it is used to comfort the reader. Phrases like “practical guide” and “keep calm” help to instil confidence that everything is under control and that the reader is being looked after. The impact of these words is bolstered by their presentation, which in this case is usually large and bold.

During this time, I imagine the brief for each of these pieces was quite concise. There was probably little time nor desire for creative flare and yet, an element of creativity can be found in each piece. Sure, most of this stuff was government issued and for that reason wouldn’t win any design awards, but in a time of great national turmoil it provided a clear way to identify, teach, help, and even accommodate commerce. That alone makes this collection worth talking about.

What do you think? Feel free to share your thoughts and comments below.
