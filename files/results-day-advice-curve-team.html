Exam results day advice from team Curve

Thursday, 22nd August, 2013

After some reminiscing about school and exams in the office, we thought we’d share some results day wisdom with the world!

So we’ve had two results days in two weeks. The team have been reminiscing about kids that open those envelopes live on air to face crushing disappointment and the tears and tribulations of exam time suddenly all over in a splash of champagne and sigh of relief from the parents. It’s also not how it used to be, with UCAS online and live result tweeting something that none of our team (not even the babyfaced Simon) can remember.

 

Pete, our MD, says…

I’ve always had a passion for art and a creative streak so I decided to do an arts diploma at Manchester before doing Fine Art at Cheltenham University. I think I did what was expected of me at school but at University I really came into my own because I was so passionate about the subject. When I graduated I started doing freelance work and the buzz from a finished job that a client was happy with (as well as a nice pay cheque!) was something I just loved. I knew at that point that I had to work for myself. Being the boss isn’t always easy but it is the most satisfying feeling when your team do a fantastic job.

The best advice I can give to young people taking those first steps towards a career is don’t shy away from a course or a job because you think it might be hard, or other people tell you it’s hard. If it’s for you then you’ll make it work and you’ll do a great job.

 

Craig, our Backend Developer, says…

I’ll be honest, I didn’t enjoy school and when it came to my A Levels I felt like I could be doing more that wasn’t on the syllabus. I didn’t pass any and I became very demotivated. I had already started teaching myself some dev but I was frustrated that nothing at school seemed to reflect what you really needed to know in the industry. I got on to an HND course and I got a diploma in my first year so they fast-tracked me on to a degree at Huddersfield Uni. When I graduated I went to work at a bank, but then a friend of mine recommended me for a job in Tenerife as I was the only designer/developer they knew. I worked out there for a year and so began my illustrious development career.

I think I would say to people leaving school and college now not to worry about how you get there, sometimes you’ll take an unexpected path but that doesn’t mean it’s wrong.

 

Tom, our Illustrator and Designer, says…

I loved art and technology I took them both for GCSE and A Levels. I did ok in my A Levels and knew I wanted to do something that combined art with computers but wasn't entirely sure of what I wanted to do exactly. I discovered the Electronic Imaging and Media Communication at Bradford University which was a sciences degree and covered lots of subjects including illustration, web design and animation. I was fortunate to find a job as a web designer when I graduated and was happy that I had a role where I could combine illustration with digital design.

My advice if you’re about to leave school or college would be to look at what you enjoy. The people that are good at what they do are good because they love it and are passionate about it!

 

Lola, our Content Strategist, says…

At the time I felt that my A Levels were an unmitigated disaster. I didn’t fail any but I didn’t get the straight As I had expected and I got into my insurance choice Uni that I hadn’t even bothered to look around. It actually turned out to be the best thing that could have happened to me. Seminars at this Uni were smaller groups and I became really confident in my ability to analyse and put my ideas forward. I became a great communicator. I graduated with a first class in English which really opened a lot of doors for me career-wise. So many people told me that it was hard to get a career in writing but weirdly my grandparents have always, always told me ‘you will write!’ Content marketing wasn’t really a career option when I first left Uni but now it’s my whole world - and I love it!

My advice if you’re leaving school or college is to not be afraid if you don’t get the results you hoped for. I was almost discouraged by certain adults asking if I was going to go through clearing to get a ‘better Uni’ but that University was the making of me.

 

Emmanuel, our Front-end Developer, says…

A mixture of teenage rebellion and ennui at school led me to start looking into development in my spare time and trying things out for myself. I did go to college but the course was terrible and barely touched upon the skills you actually need for real world development, so I read a lot and spent a lot of time doing my own projects in my spare time. Friends asked me to do some websites for them and word spread. Soon I was 18 and earning real money freelance, it was brilliant! I carried on learning and got my first job as IT support at a solicitors firm. As my portfolio expanded so did my experience and Drupal became a focus.

My advice to young people? You need to find a focus and be dedicated to that. There might be things you’re good at that aren’t really covered at school and that’s ok, but you have to be prepared to put in the hard work.

 

Simon, our UX Designer, says…

By the time I got to sixth form I was already quite into design and I really enjoyed doing Media Studies at A Level. I applied to Leeds Uni and got on to the New Media course no problem where we learned lots of different skills including coding, web accessibility and working with databases. I think sometimes people expect that if you’re a designer then you should come from a traditional art or graphic design route, but having the experience of development from my degree really informs my design choices for our clients and gives me a better understanding of how developers will work with the designs I create.

My advice is to take advice but go with your gut. You’re going to be working for a long time so doing something you feel meh about for a career won’t do you any favours. And guess what? No one is an expert in your own career path… except you!
