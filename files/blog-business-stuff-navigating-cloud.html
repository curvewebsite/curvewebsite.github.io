Navigating the Cloud

Rob Dunn
Wednesday, 21st November, 2012
Rob outlines the key considerations of file sharing and talks about two major programs that have their heads in the 'cloud'.

File sharing, everyone does it.

Whether you favour sending emails to your teams or clients or prefer to set up a digital portal, day in day out billions of megabytes are being passed from person to person every day.  So it makes sense that companies are coming up with more effective and ingenious ways to help their users share files.

Thanks to cloud sharing tools, gone are the days when emails are too big to send or teams are working off of multiple versions of the same document.

Most cloud programs are fully integrated so now that the dust has settled which program is the top dog of file sharing?

Google Drive

Formally known as Google Docs, Google Drive is a browser based file sharing platform linked to your Google login that provides you with a starting storage capacity of 5GB and reasonably priced expansion packages.  Not only does it act as an archive for all your files it also incorporates a document editor like Word, a spreadsheet like Excel and a presentation system like PowerPoint into one platform that works with almost any internet browser.

This means that you don’t have to worry about your recipients (or you) having Microsoft Office or as anyone with a browser can access the documents.  As well as this but the autosave feature ensures you never need to worry about saving if your computer suddenly has a breakdown.

Not only this but with the launch of the iphone app you can now access and edit your files on the go meaning that its easy to do those last minute tweaks even if you’re away from your desk which is great...assuming you have an iPhone that is.

Another plus is that Google Drive provides you with very granular sharing controls meaning you can grant read or write privileges for specific users for an individual document or even a whole folder of files.

Distributing documents couldn’t be easier, simply grant privileges to anyone with the web link to that file.  If it’s a confidential document, simply use the built-in sharing controls to submit an email list of intended recipients who will then be sent an email directly from Google saying that they have access to the file and then all they need to do is click on the link and login to view it.

Sounds great doesn’t it?  Well yes it is but it has its downsides to, take this quote from their small print for example...

'When you upload or otherwise submit content to our Services, you give Google (and those we work with) a worldwide license to use, host, store, reproduce, modify, create derivative works (such as those resulting from translations, adaptations or other changes we make so that your content works better with our Services), communicate, publish, publicly perform, publicly display and distribute such content. The rights you grant in this license are for the limited purpose of operating, promoting, and improving our Services, and to develop new ones.'

Don’t let this wording deter you too much though as this is the universal wording across all of Google’s products so if you’re working with highly classified information or you are an employee of Apple you may want to stay clear of Google Drive just in case.

For those of you who work with media forms, another slight downside is you can’t stream media directly from Google Drive at present.  Although this isn’t a major thing, it does mean you can’t upload all your music and video files to the cloud and then play them from there.  With the potential copyright issues this presents, I think Google can justify this one so I wouldn’t expect it to be available anytime soon.

My main issue with Google Drive is that although the sharing options are very granular, they can also be confusing; for example, from the desktop app you can only see things that are in ‘My Drive’ however you can’t move individual files that are shared with you to your My Drive folder as it moves them out of where they currently are - instead you have to drag the files host folder to My drive...confusing much?  Easiest way to remember is moving a folder creates a shortcut, moving a document... well moves the document.

My advice, if you’re not intending to use the desktop app, just use the ‘add star’ feature and same yourself some time!

Dropbox

Although you start with only half the amount of storage that Google Drive provides at 2.5GB, what I particularly like about Dropbox is that they reward you with more.  For example if you introduce a friend to the program and your friend creates an account you each get an additional 0.5GB, granted there are limited times you can do this but it does encourage you to use the program more which is a nice feature.

Both programs are widely available on nearly all platforms however unlike Google, Dropbox also works with Linux, once again for most its not a huge selling point but some of you out there are probably jumping for joy...maybe.

If you are likely to need further storage, it will cost more than Google however an advantage Dropbox has is there are no limitations on files sizes unlike Google Drive which limits you to file sizes of 10GB.  Unless you are working with some huge video or graphics files, its probably not a huge deal for you but its a nice feature to be aware of.

If you like to take the odd photo or video on your iPhone it has a handy tool of allowing you the option to auto-sync your media files so whatever happens they can all be backed up in the cloud automatically.

For desktop users the main difference between Dropbox and Google Drive is that Google’s desktop app creates shortcuts to each of the files whereas Dropbox copies the files to and from its cloud all the time meaning that even if your internet provided goes down, you can still access the latest version of the files as they exist on your computer.

When you make a change to one of those files, those changes are replicated among other Dropbox installations automatically however this does create some issues if two people are working on the same document as you’ll end up with two versions of the file and then will need to merge those changes manually.

To use Dropbox, all users have to install the program prior to being able to access its content or have to manually go to the Dropbox site and login.  For those of you that download Dropbox all the files that you have been given access to take up space on your computer so if you have limited disk space, a slow computer or a slow internet connection Dropbox may cause you some issues.

I do like the fact that I can continue to use Microsoft Office to edit my files within Dropbox as despite the functionality of Google’s programs as they are web based they do have some limitations compared to their Microsoft counterparts.  On the flipside though, unlike Google, two people can’t amend the same file at the same time as it creates duplicated files meaning you have to be a lot more aware when using Dropbox.

For viewing purposes the Dropbox app is very good, particularly on the media side however, unfortunately as each document file requires its corresponding program to edit it, it means that the mobile app can only view files so if you envisage any last minute changes Dropbox isn’t for you.

Although both programs have their pros and cons, in my experience I see Google Drive as a universal platform to easily share and work on files internally and externally across teams as well as via mobile devices whereas Dropbox I feel is much better suited to be used as a tool to synchronise your own files among multiple computers and sharing media files.
