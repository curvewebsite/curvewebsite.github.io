Payment protection insurance?

8 August 2011 | in Credit
by Nicola Hoskins, Professional Support Lawyer

The legal landscape of payment protection insurance (“PPI”) missale claims has changed dramatically over the last 9 months and is likely to continue to do so with the imminent arrival of the decision in Harrison v Black Horse from the Court of Appeal later this month.

A number of well-known cases involving Black Horse Limited were placed on the doorsteps of the courts between December 2010 and March 2011. Those cases were favourable to the lender, and ultimately provide key guidance to lenders of the application of the regulatory framework in various circumstances where PPI is sold.

Although some of these cases are not reported, it is because they are so informative that the Black Horse cases have enjoyed so much coverage. The cases of Vernalls, Speak, Buckingham, and Kerry have passed everyone’s lips in this industry, and Lord Justice Tomlinson’s eagerly awaited decision in Harrison is enjoying increasing anticipation.

But despite the numerous interesting legal arguments detailed in these cases, it is important to remember that individual PPI claims often have a limited value and involve issues appropriate to be determined at trial. In other words, they are seldom proportionate and often frustrate the parties’ absolute obligation to achieve the overriding objective.

The Black Horse cases therefore also give rise to a deterrent to claims management companies issuing PPI claims on a volume basis. All claims must be well considered in light of a lengthy and costly litigation process, which if ill thought out may give rise to an adverse costs order. The risk of an adverse costs order may be increased where parties have failed to comply with the overriding objective.

And that deterrent may well now be understood. The Financial Ombudsman Service (“FOS”) released figures last week that show a rapid increase in complaints received. In total FOS received over 56,000 complaints between April and June 2011. Or to put it another way, FOS received an astonishing 900 complaints a day. It may well surprise you that this accounted for 69% of all complaints received by FOS during that period. And what is even more surprising is that a total of 55% of the PPI complaints were upheld.

It may well be that borrowers are realising that the FOS route offers a cheaper and quicker process of dispute resolution with no risk. Essentially, the decision of FOS does not bind the complaining borrower and he or she is entitled to revert to the courts for further redress. It may well be that claims that have been rejected by FOS and issued on may give rise to a further spate of Black Horse style cases later this year.

However, it is important to remember that it was during this period the High Court formed an opinion on the British Banker’s Association judicial review. This decision caused a number of banks to adopt a policy of settling all claims and set aside huge budgets to do so.

Therefore, we can expect that FOS complaints may continue to increase and some lenders may settle claims on a commercial basis. But things aren’t going to stop there. PPI claims are a part of an economic tornado on the legal landscape, and more changes are coming.

For example a number of brokers have entered administration or been liquidated and therefore lenders may be able to direct these claims to the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (“FSCS”) in the appropriate circumstances. Broker administrations may also affect lenders strategies in dealing with Hurstanger style claims.

Lenders can only navigate the changing landscape of PPI claims by continuing to consider the legal arguments decided in the courts. And it is important that lenders continue to listen to courts and consider any relevant statistics in determining strategies.

Thankfully the courts continue to produce lender friendly decisions. HHJ Maloney QC in Cambridge County Court decided the case of Conway v Paragon Personal Finance Limited, which is unreported, favourably to the lender in July. In that case the court considered that ICOBS was not applicable to a lender where the borrower had contact with a broker and that the borrower’s vulnerability is key in determining whether commission should be disclosed.

It is expected that lenders will continue to take a considered view at the pre-action stage with transparent helpful correspondence to borrowers and their representatives stating the principles relied upon in robustly defending the speculative claim. This approach is key to ensuring that claims are managed proportionately. An increase in wasted costs orders may well be made in such circumstances where claims are issued.

The anticipation of Harrison increases.
