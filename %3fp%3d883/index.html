Trespassers will be prosecuted

12 April 2011 | in Featured Articles, Repossession
by Nicola Hoskins, Professional Support Lawyer

Being in someone else’s property without their permission is not usually a crime, but it could become one if the proposals to change the law come into effect later this year.

Squatters, and the issues surrounding squatters’ rights, have been in the media spotlight recently following a series of high profile occupations of properties in London, including one belonging to Guy Ritchie. Security firms are also reporting a rise in enquiries about providing services to absent homeowners, such as patrolling the property area and checking for signs of intrusion.

But what is the position in reality? The popular belief that ‘squatters’ rights’ exist comes from the Criminal Justice Act 1977, which makes it illegal to threaten or use violence to enter a property where someone is present and opposes the entry. The aim behind the provision was to stop landlords from using violence to evict their tenants, but it has also been used by squatters. This law does not apply to a displaced residential occupier or an intending occupier, so that someone who breaks down the door of their own home where they live is not committing a crime: provided that they can prove their status. However, occupiers of non-residential properties and those who are not displaced residential occupiers, cannot force their way into a property when someone there opposes entry, so in those situations, it becomes difficult for an owner to enforce their rights. 

There is also the issue of ‘adverse possession’, which arises when someone occupies a property or land for ten or twelve years, depending upon whether it is registered or unregistered land, after which period they can, in theory, become the owner of that property or land. But this is rare, and it also does not fit the mould of the squatter created by the media, because to be adverse, the possession has to be ‘without force, without secrecy and without permission’.       

The bottom line is that in England and Wales, squatting is actually trespass, so it is a civil matter only, unless collateral matters can be invoked: for example, breaking into a property is likely to be criminal damage, and using the utilities could amount to offences such as abstracting electricity. But the sticking point is going to be in providing the proof to make out the offence. In 1995 interim possession orders were created in order to make the process of evicting squatters quicker – such orders can be obtained through the County Court in a number of days. To give the orders teeth, they are backed with criminal sanctions so that any person who fails to comply with the order within 24 hours, or who re-enters the property within a year, commits an offence punishable with up to six months’ imprisonment. There are limitations on using the orders, however, as firstly, only possession can be claimed: there is no scope to deal with any damage caused to the property or other costs, and secondly: the police have been subject to criticism that they do not enforce the orders with sufficient tenacity.

Contrast this position with the one under Scottish Law – here, there is a provision under the Trespass (Scotland) Act 1865 which makes it an offence “to lodge in any premises or encamp on any land which is private property without the consent or permission of the owner or legal occupier”. So in this part of the UK at least, squatting is a criminal matter, although the offence is so widely drafted that the actual penalty is limited to £200, with 21 days’ imprisonment in default.

What are the plans of the Government in this area? The issue was debated in Parliament in late March 2011, and it looks likely that a consultation on the subject will be launched after the local elections in May. There appears to be cross-party support for the basic notion, but it is recognised that the debate needs to be combined with a consideration of how the issue of empty properties should be addressed. The coalition will also have to consider how criminalising squatters will fit with the cuts agenda in relation to the extra policing required, as well as the effect on a prison population it has pledged to reduce.

All in all, it would be surprising if the main thread of the proposal did not materialise into legislation, particularly given the established position in Scotland, but it looks like the devil will be found in his usual place: the balancing of the detail.
