Standard Conditions of Sale (5th Edition)

18 April 2011 | in Featured Articles, Repossession

By Thomas Bird, Technical Advisor – Repossessions

The new Standard Conditions of Sale (5th Edition), intended purely for use in relation to residential property transactions, were published by the Law Society on 1 April 2011 and include: 

  • A new Conveyancing Protocol
  • Standard Conditions of Sale (5th Edition) – replacing the 4th Edition
  • A revised code for completion by post
  • A revised Completion Information and Requisitions on Title (form TA13), now named Completion Information and Undertakings

The Conveyancing Protocol is aimed at a standard sale and purchase situation, where the sale is by a property owner.  As such, whilst a working knowledge of the new Protocol is essential, the majority of solicitors acting for lender clients will not adopt it in their repossession sales matters, other than in relation to general professional conduct. 

Similarly, the new code for completion by post, and form TA13, whilst of interest, will be rarely used in a sale on behalf of a mortgagee in possession, due to the seller’s lack of personal knowledge of the property being sold.

Of more relevance to repossession sales matters are the Standard Conditions of Sale (5th Edition) and in particular the differences to the 4th Edition.

Whilst the new Conditions do not impose any more onerous terms on a seller than their predecessors, a number of significant changes to the Standard Conditions will have a knock on effect on lenders’ Special Conditions.  These include:

  • Transference of risk to the buyer from exchange, rather than at completion as under the 4th Edition.
  • Replacement of the word ‘chattels’ with ‘contents’.
  • The need for evidence of payment of any monies that are to be apportioned upon completion.
  • Deposit and balance of purchase funds to be transferred to and from a conveyancer’s bank account.
  • Exclusion of the implied covenant under s.4 Law of Property (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1994 in relation to the physical state of the property.

Many of these changes reflect the position already adopted by many lender clients in repossession sales cases, via their Special Conditions, and therefore, in order to maintain the status quo a re-drafting of their Special Conditions will be necessary.
