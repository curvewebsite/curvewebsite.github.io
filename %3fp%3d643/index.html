Conditional Fee Arrangements still offer lenders a valid proposition

9 September 2010 | in Dispute Resolution
by Rachel Hutton, Partner & Head of Dispute Resolution

Lord Justice Jackson recently criticised Conditional Fee Agreements (CFA’s) claiming that they reward inefficiency and are an easy way out.  But whatever negative comments there are of CFA’s, they are still a valid and legitimate proposition for lenders making a claim.

Deemed too good to be true, CFA’s allow certainty in relation to costs of litigation for the client. They mean that the client can be comfortable that it will not have to pay its legal costs for pursuing the litigation. They do not, however, protect against the risk of having to pay the other side’s costs should the client lose. It is a case of the lawyers sharing the risk with the client, on the basis that if successful the lawyer can obtain enhanced fees, a “success fee” which it will claim from the other side as part of the costs.

After the Event Insurance (ATE) can be obtained to ensure that in unsuccessful cases, the client will not have to pay the other side’s costs and disbursements. If the claim is successful, the premium can be recovered from the other side, equally, if the lender does lose, the costs are paid by the insurer and the premium is covered by the policy.

Without doubt CFA’s are facing increased scrutiny from the industry because they allow the lawyer to recover a success fee.  Proposals for reform are in the pipeline, but with the recent change in government and the Ministry of Justice carrying out its own impact analysis, the date and full effect of new legislation is uncertain.  Legal advisers should still, therefore, be offering lenders CFA’s and looking to share the risk of litigation until the law changes.
