Consumer insurance law – affecting all insurance claims

11 May 2010 | in Credit
by Paul Loveridge, Associate Solicitor

The English and Scottish Law Commissions (“the Commissions”) have submitted to Parliament a joint report (“Report”), which considers what should be discussed between a customer and insurer during the pre-contractual period in consumer insurance contracts. It states that the current law governing the pre-contractual period in consumer insurance contracts is unfair, lacks transparency and is outdated. 

The Report includes a draft Bill entitled The Consumer Insurance (Disclosure and Representations) Bill (“Bill”) and recommends it is enacted.

Currently, the pre-contractual period in consumer insurance contracts is governed by the principles in the Marine Insurance Act 1906 (“the Act”). The Act imposes upon the customer a duty to disclose all material facts to the insurer in order to allow the insurer to assess the risk and price in offering a policy to the customer. In the event that the customer fails to disclose all material facts to the insurer, the insurer is entitled to avoid the policy and refuse any claim made under it.

The Commissions state that the Act creates too wide a duty on the customer, which gives rise to question the transparency of policies and may lead to unfairness. They argue that the duty by its very nature is a trap for consumers who are largely unaware that such an obligation applies to them.

However, the main argument advanced by the Commissions is that an insurer may seek to avoid a policy by introducing any undisclosed fact it considers reasonable, even though the claim does not involve that fact. They are in effect claiming that a significant number of what may be reasonably considered valid policies are being refused unfairly each year, and that consumers deserve protection. For instance, policies in which the consumer has acted honestly, but made a simple mistake.

An example is provided in the Report in which a policyholder’s claim was refused because he was convicted of a common assault during the term of an insurance contract. The insurer stated that the policy was described as “a monthly contract” and asserted that the policyholder was under a duty to report all material facts on a monthly basis. This matter was heard before the Financial Services Ombudsman (“FSO”), who rejected the insurer’s assertion.

The Commissions further use that example to advance a separate argument that the current law is outdated as it is at odds with the approach of the Financial Services Ombudsman and also the principles regulated by the Financial Services Authority (“FSA”). The Commission describes as “nonsense” the fact that an insurer could choose to reject a policy on the current law under the Act, yet face a fine by the FSA or ordered by the FOS to honour the policy.

The Bill abolishes the duty of good faith imposed on the consumer. It is replaced with a duty for the consumer to respond honestly and with reasonable care to questions asked by the insurer in entering into a consumer insurance contract. It also provides statutory remedies for misrepresentation and non-disclosure made by the customer during the pre-contractual period.

In considering whether the customer has discharged the duty to exercise reasonable care, a Court must consider various factors such as the clarity and relevance of the insurer’s questions, the type of policy acquired or sought, and any external factors such as associated documentation provided by the insurer or the involvement of an agent (The role of an Agent is also considered in the Report and dealt with in the Bill, but is not considered in this article).

 It is for the insurer to prove that the consumer’s answers were deliberate, reckless or careless to amount to a misrepresentation which induced the insurer to offer or enter into the contract. On the insurer evidencing the misrepresentation the Court will do one of two things.

In the event that the consumer knowingly answers a question with a deliberate or reckless misrepresentation a Court will always consider that the consumer failed to discharge their duty to the insurer, regardless of those various factors. The insurer may avoid the policy, refuse any claim, and keep the premium paid.

However, if the Court considers that the misrepresentation made by the consumer was careless it will grant relief to the insurer proportionately by allowing the insurer to state what it would have done in the event that the misrepresentation was disclosed at the time of entering into or proposing to enter into the contract.

Therefore, if the policy or certain parts of it would have been refused by the insurer at the time of entering into or proposing to enter into the contract, the Court may either allow the insurer to avoid the policy in full or part. However, if the insurer would have continued to offer a policy the court would award the insurer a counterclaim for damages in any increase in the premium to be set off against the claim.

Section 1 of the Bill provides the main definitions. ‘Consumer’ is defined as ‘a person who enters into a consumer insurance contract, or proposes to do so’. ‘Consumer insurance contract’ is defined as ‘a contract of insurance entered into by an individual wholly or mainly for purposes unrelated to the individual’s trade, business and profession’. An ‘insurer’ is defined as ‘a person who is, or would become, the other party to a consumer insurance contract’.

It is envisaged in the Bill that it would have strict application to all consumer contracts. Therefore, like the Consumer Credit Act 1974 (as amended), the parties to the contract could not agree to contract out of the provisions of the Bill and avoid its application. There is nothing to state that parties cannot contract into the Bill, however the Commissions are currently considering separate recommendations to modify business insurance law, which should be available later this year.
