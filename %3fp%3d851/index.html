Can an honest solicitor be liable for the losses caused by a fraudster?

21 March 2011 | in Featured Articles, Mortgage Fraud
by Maggie Batts, Associate Solicitor

This was the question considered by the High Court in Lloyds TSB Bank plc v Markandan & Uddin [2010] EWHC 2517 (Ch 7).

The defendant firm of solicitors acted for the purchaser of a residential property in London.  They also acted for the lender and paid over the advance to the sellers’ solicitors, having signed an unqualified certificate of title.

It turned out that the entire transaction was a sham: the “purchaser” was a fraudster, the firm of solicitors acting for the “sellers” did not exist and the true owners of the property denied all knowledge of the matter.

The court accepted that the defendant solicitors had acted honestly and played no part in the fraud.  However, they had acted in breach of trust when they paid away the advance without receiving the documents necessary to register the title, or alternatively, a solicitor’s undertaking to provide those documents.  

For the same reasons, the solicitors were not entitled to relief under the Trustee Act 1925.  Although there was no challenge to their honesty, they had not acted reasonably when they released the advance without having received the necessary documents.  They also failed to establish the credentials of the sellers’ purported solicitors.

The lender was entitled to recover the whole of the advance (more than £700,000) plus interest.   No deduction could be made for alleged contributory negligence in a claim for breach of trust and there was no question that the loss should be calculated by any other method.
